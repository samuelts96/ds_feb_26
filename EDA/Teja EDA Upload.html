KEY INSIGHTS

1. Price is Strongly Correlated with Age (0.79)
This is the strongest relationship in the matrix. As age increases, price purchased increases significantly.

2. Salary Strongly Correlates with Total Salary (0.62)
Logical and expected. Individual income is a major component of household income.

3. Partner Salary Very Strongly Correlates with Total Salary (0.82)
This is the strongest internal income relationship. Partner income heavily drives household earning power.

4. Price Has Moderate Correlation with Salary (0.39) and Total Salary (0.35)
Income influences price, but not as strongly as age.

5. No_of_Dependents Has Weak or Negative Correlations
It has minimal relationship with salary and price. Slight negative relationship with price (-0.14).

6. Age Has Moderate Correlation with Salary (0.61)
Income increases with age, which is expected due to career progression.

BUSINESS IMPLICATIONS

1. Age Is the Strongest Driver of Purchase Value
Customers buy more expensive properties as they grow older. Target higher-value products toward older segments.

2. Career Progression Drives Spending Power
Since age and salary are strongly linked, pricing strategy should consider career stage, not just raw income.

3. Dual-Income Households Are Financially Powerful
Partner salary strongly impacts total salary. Marketing should target dual-income families for higher-ticket offerings.

4. Income Alone Does Not Fully Explain Price
Since salary correlation with price is moderate (not very high), other factors like age, lifestyle, and financial maturity matter.

5. Dependents Are Not a Strong Price Driver
Family size does not significantly increase property price in this dataset. Avoid over-weighting dependents in pricing models.(Necessary)

6. Total Salary and Partner Salary Create Multicollinearity Risk
Because they are highly correlated (0.82), including both in predictive models without control may distort results.

Final Insights and Business Implications

 
Top 3 Important Key Insights

Age and Spending Correlation: There is a clear influence of customer age on car spending behavior. While there is overlap across age groups, spending patterns generally shift as customers get older.

Purchasing Patterns by Life Stage: Younger customers are more likely to purchase budget-friendly or entry-level vehicles, whereas premium and luxury cars are predominantly targeted toward and purchased by older customers.

Income Influence: The data suggests that car prices are influenced by income growth over a customer’s lifetime, with mid-priced cars being most popular among customers in their 30s and early 40s.
 
Top 3 Business Implications

Targeted Marketing and Segmentation: Austo should use age-based segmentation to improve product positioning. Marketing messages should be tailored to evolve with the customer's specific life stage and age.

Strategic Product Distribution: The company should focus on targeting entry-level and budget cars specifically toward younger demographics, while luxury models should be marketed to older, higher-income segments.

Financial Customization: Offering flexible financing and EMI (Equated Monthly Installment) options is critical for capturing the younger buyer market, who may have lower immediate liquid capital despite their interest in purchasing.

 

Conclusion

 
The analysis concludes that the U.S. car market for Austo is heavily segmented by age and income levels. By aligning their marketing strategies, product placements, and financing options with these demographic realities, Austo can better "crack the code" of the market to drive smarter production and expansion decisions.
